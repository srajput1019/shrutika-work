The gap between what a referral arrives with and what the payer requires.

Tennr's RaeLM reads an inbound referral and validates it against payer criteria to cut denials — auto-completing the clean ones. But the referral that doesn't clear lands on a human, who has to figure out, fast, exactly what is missing to get to yes. This is that surface: the payer's medical-necessity checklist on one side, what RaeLM extracted versus what's still missing on the other, and an auto-drafted request to the referring provider for every gap — turning "denied for missing documentation" into a guided path to an approved authorization.

Why this surface

Tennr's whole promise is validating a referral against payer requirements to cut denials — and its published result is a 98% drop in first-time denials. The referrals RaeLM can't auto-clear are exactly the ones with a documentation gap, and closing that gap is where same-day intake is actually won or lost. Talkiatry cut fax processing from 24–48 hours to ~1 minute and replaced 1–3 manual follow-ups per missing-info case with automated outreach — the "Communications Coordination" loop this workspace makes visible.

The three flagged referrals span Tennr's real verticals — sleep / DME, home health, and infusion — to show one gap-closing pattern that generalizes across payer criteria, not a single hard-coded screen.

Grounded in my own work

For Capital Market Solutions (RSMS) I designed a regulatory exception-management console — the single view where a compliance team investigates and resolves flagged cases — which cut manual reporting errors 25%. The "what fired, what's missing, close the loop" pattern here is the same discipline.

On the UnitedHealthcare ethical-AI pilot I designed the Medicare coverage-determination and billing UX, mapping the patient journey and embedding human oversight at every decision point.
