At Norm, a human's sign-off is the product

Norm turns regulations into agents that reason explainably, and its DDQ and RFP product drafts answers with Evidence-Based Drafting, reuses prior approved language, and gets sharper through Continuous Learning. But the deliverable a financial-services client pays for is the moment an attorney signs. This is a concept for that sign-off surface, where Supervisory AI hands a drafted answer to a reviewer who must decide, fast, whether it is defensible.

Three design decisions in here

1. The citation is one click from the claim. Evidence-Based Drafting is only trustworthy if the attorney can verify a line without leaving the answer. Hover a cite and its source, date, and freshness are right there, the difference between "found" and "re-researched."

2. Supervisory AI flags its own doubt, legibly. A confidence score is not enough; the reviewer needs to see why it is unsure, a stale source, a policy that changed, a first-of-kind question with no reusable answer.

3. Every sign-off is Continuous Learning. Approve, edit, or reject is labeled data. An edit becomes a reusable answer; a reject retires a stale source. The queue shrinks as the agent learns what this reviewer trusts.

Why I built this

My capstone, Qlarc, is the closest thing to Norm I have shipped: an AI-governance platform that turns the evidence a vendor already has into proof an enterprise buyer can evaluate, frameworks like SR 11-7, DORA, and the EU AI Act, evidence-pack prep cut from 15 to 20 days down to hours, 65 percent of answers found rather than rewritten. On Swaayata, at Innovation Hacks, I built the oversight and override surface for an autonomous system; on a CMS regulatory program, an exception review queue that cut manual errors 25 percent.

The sign-off surface is where all of that meets. I would want the first conversation to be about how the reviewer's confidence scales as Norm does.
