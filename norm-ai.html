Three design decisions in here

1. The citation is one click from the claim. Evidence-Based Drafting is only trustworthy if the attorney can verify a line without leaving the answer. Hover a cite and its source, date, and freshness are right there — the difference between "found" and "re-researched."

2. Supervisory AI flags its own doubt, legibly. A confidence score isn't enough; the reviewer needs to see why it's unsure — a stale source, a policy that changed, a first-of-kind question with no reusable answer.

3. Every sign-off is Continuous Learning. Approve, edit, or reject is labeled data. An edit becomes a reusable answer; a reject retires a stale source. The queue shrinks as the agent learns what this reviewer trusts.

Why I built this

My capstone, Qlarc, is the closest thing to Norm I've shipped: an AI-governance platform that turns the evidence a vendor already has into proof an enterprise buyer can evaluate — frameworks like SR 11-7, DORA, and the EU AI Act, evidence-pack prep cut from 15-20 days to hours, 65% of answers found rather than rewritten. On Swaayata (Innovation Hacks) I built the oversight-and-override surface for an autonomous system; on a CMS regulatory program, an exception-review queue that cut manual errors 25%.

The sign-off surface is where all of that meets. I'd want the first conversation to be about how the reviewer's confidence scales as Norm does.
